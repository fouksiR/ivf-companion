# Between Cycles, Considering Stopping, and Other Paths
[STAGE: wtf_appointment, between_cycles, considering_stopping, donor_journey]

## The follow-up appointment (the "WTF" appointment)
[EDUCATION: wtf_appointment]

After a failed cycle, your specialist will want to review what happened and discuss what to do next. Patients sometimes call this the "WTF appointment" — and honestly, that name captures the feeling perfectly.

**What your specialist will typically review:**
- How your body responded to the medications (was the dose right? enough follicles?)
- How many eggs were retrieved and their quality
- Fertilisation and embryo development — was there a pattern in the attrition?
- The embryo that was transferred — its quality, and the transfer itself
- Your uterine lining — was it thick enough? Were there any issues on ultrasound?
- Whether genetic testing of embryos (PGT-A) might be recommended next time

**Questions you might want to ask:**
- Do you know why this cycle didn't work?
- What would you change for the next cycle?
- Are there additional tests you'd recommend before trying again?
- What are our realistic chances if we try again?
- Should we consider genetic testing of embryos?
- How long should we wait before the next cycle?

**The emotional reality:** This appointment can feel clinical when you're still raw. It's okay to bring your partner or a support person. It's okay to cry. It's okay to ask them to slow down or explain things differently. Your specialist has had this conversation many times — they understand.

## Giving your body and mind time to recover
[EDUCATION: between_rest]

Between cycles, your body and mind both need time. How much time is different for everyone, and there's no minimum or maximum.

**Your body:**
- Your ovaries need time to return to baseline size after stimulation
- Most specialists recommend waiting at least one full menstrual cycle before starting again
- Some people benefit from 2-3 months, especially if OHSS was a concern
- Your hormone levels need to normalise

**Your mind:**
- The emotional toll of a failed cycle doesn't disappear on a schedule
- Some people feel ready to try again almost immediately — the "doing something" feels better than waiting
- Others need months to process before they can face it again
- Both approaches are valid. Neither is better

**Things that help during this time:**
- Reconnect with parts of your life that IVF has pushed aside — friends, hobbies, travel, your relationship as something other than "people trying to have a baby"
- Physical recovery: gentle exercise, good nutrition, reducing alcohol, sleep
- Emotional recovery: counselling, journaling, time with people who care about you
- Decide your own boundaries about when and how much to talk about IVF with others

**A note on relationships:** IVF puts extraordinary strain on couples. Between cycles can be a time to reconnect — or it can be a time when cracks become more visible. If your relationship is struggling, that's not unusual, and couples counselling can help.

## The courage of considering stopping
[EDUCATION: considering_stopping]

There may come a point where you start wondering: "Should we stop?" This question is one of the hardest in the entire IVF journey, and there is no universal right answer.

**You might be considering stopping because:**
- Multiple failed cycles have taken an enormous toll
- The financial burden has become unsustainable
- Your mental or physical health is suffering
- Your relationship is under too much strain
- You feel like you've given it your best and it's time to explore other paths
- Something inside you is saying "enough" — and that instinct matters

**What stopping does NOT mean:**
- It doesn't mean you failed. You fought incredibly hard
- It doesn't mean you "gave up." Choosing to stop takes more courage than continuing sometimes
- It doesn't mean you won't be a parent — it means this particular path isn't the one
- It doesn't mean you weren't strong enough. You were strong every single day of this

**What it might mean:**
- Space to grieve and heal
- Freedom from the cycle of hope and disappointment
- The beginning of exploring other options (donor eggs, donor embryos, surrogacy, adoption, or choosing a life without children)
- A different kind of brave

**Getting support:** This decision deserves professional support — a fertility counsellor can help you and your partner process what you're feeling and make a decision you can both live with. There's no pressure and no timeline.

## Exploring other paths — donor, surrogacy, adoption
[EDUCATION: donor_journey]

If you're considering donor eggs, donor sperm, donor embryos, surrogacy, or adoption, you're opening a door to a different kind of parenthood. That door comes with its own complex emotions.

**Donor eggs or sperm:**
- Grief about losing the genetic connection is real and valid
- Many people find that once they process this grief, they feel genuinely excited about this path
- The child will still be yours — carried in your body (with donor eggs), raised by you, loved by you
- Donor conception is more common than most people realise

**Surrogacy:**
- In Australia, only altruistic surrogacy is legal (the surrogate cannot be paid beyond expenses)
- Finding a surrogate is often the hardest part — it's deeply personal
- The legal and emotional process is complex but navigable with the right support

**Adoption:**
- This is a completely separate journey with its own process, timeline, and emotional landscape
- It's not a consolation prize — it's a valid, beautiful path to parenthood
- The process in Australia can be lengthy, and it helps to research thoroughly

**What all these paths have in common:** They require letting go of one dream to make space for another. That letting go is a grief in itself, and it deserves time and support.

## Life after IVF — whatever that looks like
[EDUCATION: life_after]

Whether you end IVF with a baby, a pregnancy, or neither — you've been changed by this experience. And that deserves acknowledgement.

**If you're pregnant or have a baby:**
- The anxiety of IVF doesn't always disappear with a positive result or even with a baby in your arms
- IVF parents sometimes carry a vigilance that spontaneous-conception parents don't understand
- Post-IVF pregnancy and parenthood can feel different, and that's okay
- You don't owe anyone your IVF story, but you also don't need to hide it

**If you're moving on without a biological child:**
- Grief may come in waves for years, especially around trigger dates (due dates, retrieval anniversaries, baby announcements)
- Building a meaningful, full life without children is possible and valid
- Your identity is not defined by your reproductive history
- Finding your community — people who understand — can make an enormous difference

**For everyone:**
- You went through something incredibly hard, and you deserve to be proud of yourself
- The lessons IVF teaches — about resilience, vulnerability, hope, and loss — don't disappear
- It's okay to look back with sadness, anger, gratitude, or all of the above
- You are more than this experience, even when it doesn't feel that way
