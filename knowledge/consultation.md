# First Consultation
[STAGE: consultation]

## What to expect at your first fertility appointment
[EDUCATION: consultation_what_to_expect]

Your first visit to a fertility specialist can feel overwhelming — you might be nervous, hopeful, scared, or all three at once. That's completely normal.

Here's what usually happens: your specialist will ask about your medical history, your menstrual cycles, how long you've been trying, and any relevant health issues. They'll probably order some blood tests and an ultrasound scan. If you have a partner, they'll likely need a semen analysis too.

The appointment itself usually takes about 30-45 minutes. Some people find it helpful to bring a notebook or their phone to take notes — there's a lot of information and it's hard to remember everything.

One thing many people don't expect: you might not get a clear "plan" on the first visit. Your specialist often needs to see all the test results before recommending a path forward. That waiting can feel frustrating, but it means they're being thorough.

It's okay to cry during the appointment. It's okay to feel nothing. It's okay to leave and realise you forgot to ask half your questions. That's what follow-up calls and the next appointment are for.

## Questions worth asking your specialist
[EDUCATION: consultation_questions]

You don't need to ask all of these — pick the ones that matter most to you. And remember, there are no stupid questions.

About your situation:
- Based on what you know so far, what do you think might be going on?
- What tests do I need, and what will they tell us?
- Is there anything I can do right now to improve our chances?

About treatment options:
- What treatment path do you recommend, and why?
- What are the realistic chances of success for someone like me?
- How many cycles do you typically recommend trying?

About the practical side:
- How long does a typical cycle take from start to finish?
- How many appointments will I need, and can I still work during treatment?
- What does this cost, and what's covered by Medicare?

About support:
- Do you have a fertility counsellor I can speak to?
- Are there any support groups you recommend?
- What happens if this doesn't work — what are our other options?

## Understanding fertility testing
[EDUCATION: consultation_testing]

The tests your specialist orders might feel like a lot, but each one gives an important piece of the puzzle. Here's what the common ones mean in plain language:

**Blood tests (for you):**
- **AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone):** This gives a picture of your ovarian reserve — roughly, how many eggs you have left. It's NOT a fertility score. A lower AMH doesn't mean you can't get pregnant; it means there may be fewer eggs to work with, so timing matters more.
- **FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone):** This is the hormone that tells your ovaries to get to work each month. If it's high, your body is working harder to stimulate your ovaries — which can mean the reserve is lower.
- **Oestradiol, LH, Progesterone:** These help build a picture of your cycle and whether you're ovulating normally.
- **Thyroid function, Vitamin D, Iron:** These seem unrelated but can all affect fertility and pregnancy outcomes.

**Ultrasound scan:**
Your specialist will do a transvaginal ultrasound (an internal scan — it's a bit uncomfortable but not usually painful). They're counting your antral follicles — the small fluid-filled sacs in your ovaries that each contain an immature egg. The number gives a snapshot of your ovarian reserve that complements the AMH blood test.

**Semen analysis (for your partner):**
This checks sperm count, how well they swim (motility), and what they look like (morphology). It's one of the most important tests — male factor is involved in about 40-50% of couples having trouble conceiving. Your partner might feel awkward about it, and that's normal too.

Try not to google your results before discussing them with your specialist. Numbers without context can cause unnecessary panic.
