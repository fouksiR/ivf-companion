# Fertilisation & Embryo Development
[STAGE: fertilisation_report, embryo_development]

## Understanding your fertilisation report
[EDUCATION: fert_report]

The day after retrieval, you'll get the call that many people describe as one of the most anxious moments of the whole process: your fertilisation report.

Here's what the numbers mean:

**Eggs retrieved → Mature eggs:** Not all eggs retrieved are mature enough to be fertilised. Only mature eggs (called MII eggs) can be used. Losing some here is completely normal — typically 70-85% of retrieved eggs are mature.

**Mature eggs → Fertilised:** Of the mature eggs, typically 60-80% will fertilise normally. "Normal fertilisation" means the egg and sperm joined properly, with two pronuclei visible (this is called 2PN).

**So if you had 10 eggs retrieved**, you might have 7-8 mature, and 5-6 fertilised. That's a normal, healthy result — even though it feels like a big drop.

**What "didn't fertilise" means:** Sometimes eggs don't fertilise, and it doesn't always mean something is wrong. It can be the egg, the sperm, or just biology being biology. It's not your fault.

**ICSI vs conventional IVF:** If your clinic used ICSI (where they inject a single sperm directly into each egg), fertilisation rates may be slightly different. Your embryologist can explain what method was used and why.

The hardest part of this call is that you're watching the numbers drop. Try to remember: you only need one good embryo. Quality matters more than quantity.

## The attrition curve — why numbers drop and what it means
[EDUCATION: embryo_attrition]

Between fertilisation and transfer or freeze, you'll lose some embryos. This is called the "attrition curve" and it happens in every single IVF cycle, for everyone. It's not a sign that something went wrong — it's biology doing what biology does.

Here's a typical progression:

- **Day 1:** Fertilisation check — you learn how many fertilised normally
- **Day 2-3:** The fertilised eggs start dividing into cells. Some will stall or divide abnormally. It's common to lose 10-30% here
- **Day 5-6 (blastocyst stage):** The embryos that keep developing reach a stage called "blastocyst" — a more complex structure with about 100 cells. Typically, 30-60% of fertilised eggs make it to this stage

**Why does this happen?** Most embryos that stop developing have genetic abnormalities that would prevent a healthy pregnancy. This natural selection happens in natural conception too — you just don't usually see it. In IVF, you're watching it happen in real time, and that can be emotionally brutal.

**What if you have fewer than expected?** It's a grief, and it's valid. Each drop feels like losing a chance. But remember: the embryos that do make it through are the strong ones. One good blastocyst can become your baby.

**What if you have more than expected?** That's genuinely wonderful. Extra blastocysts can be frozen for future use — giving you more chances without another stimulation cycle.

## What embryo grading means (and doesn't mean)
[EDUCATION: embryo_grading]

Your clinic will "grade" your embryos — and this can become a source of enormous anxiety. Let's put it in perspective.

**What the letters and numbers mean:**

For blastocysts (Day 5-6), grading typically uses three parts:
1. **A number (1-6):** How expanded the blastocyst is. Higher = more expanded = further along in development
2. **A letter for the inner cell mass (A, B, or C):** This is the part that becomes the baby. A = lots of tightly packed cells, B = slightly fewer or less organised, C = very few cells
3. **A letter for the trophectoderm (A, B, or C):** This is the part that becomes the placenta. Same scale

So a "4AA" is a fully expanded blastocyst with excellent inner cell mass and trophectoderm. A "3BB" is a bit less expanded with good (not excellent) cell quality.

**What you need to know:** Grading is subjective — different embryologists may grade the same embryo slightly differently. And the grade is NOT a baby score. "Average" grade embryos become healthy babies all the time. A BB embryo is not a "B student" — it's a perfectly viable embryo.

The most important thing is that the embryo reached blastocyst stage at all. That already tells you it passed the biggest test.

## Waiting for daily embryo updates
[EDUCATION: embryo_daily_updates]

Some clinics give daily updates. Others only call on key days (Day 1, Day 3, Day 5). Either way, this waiting period can feel excruciating.

**Some things that help:**
- Ask your clinic upfront when they'll call and stick to that expectation
- If you know the update comes at 10am, try to keep the morning busy
- Have someone with you when you take the call if you can
- Write down what they tell you — you might not remember details later
- Ask the embryologist to repeat anything you didn't understand

**What you can't control:** How your embryos develop. There is literally nothing you can do right now to influence what's happening in the lab. That powerlessness is one of the hardest parts of IVF.

**What you can control:** How you take care of yourself while you wait. Be gentle. Distract yourself. This is a good time to lean on the people around you.
