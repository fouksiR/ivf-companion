# Results, Loss, and Moving Forward
[STAGE: result_day, positive_result, negative_result, chemical_pregnancy, miscarriage, failed_cycle_acute, failed_cycle_processing]

## Understanding your beta HCG result
[EDUCATION: result_beta_hcg]

The blood test that determines whether the transfer worked measures a hormone called beta-hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin). Here's what the numbers mean:

**A positive result:** Generally, a beta-hCG level above 25 mIU/mL is considered positive. But the number itself matters less than what it does next — your clinic will usually repeat the test 48-72 hours later to check that the level is rising appropriately (ideally doubling or close to it).

**A negative result:** A level below 5 mIU/mL means the embryo did not implant. There are no ambiguous "maybes" with a negative blood test, unlike home pregnancy tests.

**The grey zone:** A low positive (5-25 mIU/mL) can be the hardest result — it might be a very early pregnancy that needs monitoring, or it might be a chemical pregnancy. Your clinic will retest and guide you through what the numbers mean.

**About the number itself:** Please try not to compare your hCG number with others online. Normal hCG levels at the same stage of pregnancy can vary enormously — from 50 to 500+ — and still result in healthy pregnancies. The trend (rising, stable, or falling) matters more than any single number.

## Positive result — when joy comes with fear
[EDUCATION: result_positive]

If your test is positive: congratulations. Genuinely. You've achieved something extraordinary.

And also: you might not feel the way you expected. Many IVF patients describe the positive result not as pure joy, but as the beginning of a new kind of anxiety. After everything you've been through, it can feel impossible to trust that this is real.

**What you might feel:**
- Joy mixed with disbelief — "Is this actually happening?"
- Fear that something will go wrong — after so much loss of control, trusting a good outcome feels dangerous
- Guilt about not being "happy enough" — especially if you know others who are still trying
- Hypervigilance — analysing every symptom, terrified of bleeding
- A strange emptiness — the thing you've been working towards has happened, and now what?

**All of these are normal.** IVF pregnancy is psychologically different from spontaneous pregnancy. You've earned your anxiety. But try to also let moments of joy in when they come.

**What happens next:**
- Your clinic will monitor you closely for the first 6-8 weeks with blood tests and early scans
- You'll typically have your first ultrasound around 6-7 weeks to check for a heartbeat
- You'll continue progesterone support as directed
- At some point (usually 8-12 weeks), you'll transition from your fertility clinic to an obstetrician

## Negative result — your grief is real
[EDUCATION: result_negative]

A negative pregnancy test after IVF is a profound loss. It doesn't matter that there was never a visible pregnancy — you lost a hope, a plan, a possible future. That grief is real and valid.

**There is no right way to feel:**
- Devastated, angry, numb, relieved it's over (for now), guilty, broken, determined, empty — all of these are legitimate responses
- You might feel fine for a few days and then fall apart. Grief comes in waves
- You might feel angry at your body, at the universe, at people who get pregnant easily
- You might feel nothing at all — emotional shutdown is a protective response

**What doesn't help (but people will say anyway):**
- "At least you can try again" — you're not ready to hear this yet
- "Everything happens for a reason" — no, it doesn't. Sometimes things are just unfair
- "Just relax and it'll happen" — this is not a relaxation problem
- "Maybe you should adopt" — that's a completely different conversation

**What does help:**
- Letting yourself grieve without a timeline
- Telling your partner or support person what you need (space? company? someone to cry with?)
- Giving yourself permission to take a few days off — from work, from decisions, from the world
- Knowing that this result doesn't predict future results
- Professional support — a fertility counsellor has heard it all and can help you process

You don't need to be strong right now. You don't need to have a plan. You just need to get through today.

## Chemical pregnancy — a special kind of loss
[EDUCATION: result_chemical]

A chemical pregnancy is when the initial blood test is positive, but the hCG levels fail to rise properly or start falling — meaning the pregnancy ended very early, often before it could be seen on ultrasound.

**Why it's uniquely painful:**
- You had a positive test. You let yourself hope. Maybe you told someone. And then it was taken away
- Some people feel it's "not a real loss" because there was never a scan or a heartbeat — but it absolutely is
- The whiplash between joy and grief can be disorienting
- You might feel your body betrayed you — it said "yes" and then said "no"

**What happened:** In most chemical pregnancies, the embryo had a genetic abnormality that prevented it from developing further. This is not something you caused. It's not about what you did or didn't do after transfer.

**The silver lining your specialist might mention:** A chemical pregnancy shows that your body can achieve implantation — the embryo did attach, briefly. For some patients, this is actually a positive sign for future cycles, even though it doesn't feel like one right now.

You're allowed to grieve this. You're allowed to be angry about it. And you're allowed to take whatever time you need before thinking about what comes next.

## Miscarriage after IVF — compounded grief
[EDUCATION: result_miscarriage]

Losing a pregnancy after IVF carries a weight that is hard to describe to people who haven't experienced it. It's not just the loss of a pregnancy — it's the loss of everything you went through to get there.

**The grief is layered:**
- Grief for the pregnancy itself
- Grief for the time, money, physical toll, and emotional investment of the IVF cycle
- Fear that this will keep happening
- Anger at the unfairness — after everything, this too?
- Loneliness — many people don't understand the IVF layer of this loss

**Physical recovery:** Your body needs time to recover, both from the pregnancy and from the IVF medications. Your clinic will guide you on what to expect physically and when it's safe to consider another cycle if you choose to.

**Emotional recovery has no timeline.** Some people want to try again immediately. Others need months. Both are valid. There is no "correct" pace of healing.

**Getting support:** This is a time when professional support can make an enormous difference. A fertility counsellor or psychologist who specialises in pregnancy loss can help you process what's happened. You don't have to do this alone.

## The days right after a failed cycle
[EDUCATION: failed_acute]

The first few days after a failed cycle are often the hardest of the entire IVF journey. Here's what you need to hear:

**You don't have to decide anything right now.** Not whether to try again, not what to change, not what to do with your life. Those decisions can wait.

**Your body needs physical recovery.** You've been through hormonal medications, possibly a procedure, and emotional stress. Rest is not laziness — it's necessity.

**You might feel:**
- Like you can't get out of bed — so don't, for a day or two
- Like you need to keep busy — that's okay too
- Angry at pregnant women, baby announcements, and social media — consider muting or avoiding these for now
- Like your relationship is strained — IVF puts enormous pressure on couples
- Like you're the only person in the world going through this — you're not, even though it feels that way

**Practical things:**
- Let your workplace know you need a couple of days if possible — you don't need to explain why
- Accept help if it's offered
- Eat and drink, even if you don't feel like it
- Move your body gently when you're ready — even a short walk outside
- Cry as much as you need to

## Processing a failed cycle — making meaning
[EDUCATION: failed_processing]

After the acute grief passes (and it will, gradually, even if it doesn't feel like it), you might find yourself trying to make sense of what happened.

**Common questions:**
- "Why didn't it work?" — Sometimes there's a clear answer (embryo genetics, implantation failure, lining issues). Often there isn't. Unexplained failure is one of the hardest things to sit with
- "Was it something I did?" — No. Failed implantation is almost never caused by something the patient did or didn't do
- "Should we try again?" — This is a deeply personal decision that involves your heart, your body, your finances, and your relationship. There's no universally right answer
- "How many cycles should we try?" — Your specialist can help with the medical side of this question, but the emotional and financial sides are yours

**What helps during this time:**
- Talking to your partner about where you each are — you may be in different places, and that's okay
- Booking a follow-up appointment with your specialist (sometimes called a "WTF appointment") to review what happened and discuss options
- Connecting with others who understand — support groups, online communities, or a counsellor
- Giving yourself permission to feel good on good days without guilt

**A truth about resilience:** You are stronger than you think. Not because this experience hasn't hurt — it has. But because you're still here, still reading this, still open to what comes next. That takes extraordinary courage.
