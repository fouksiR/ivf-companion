# Egg Retrieval
[STAGE: before_retrieval, retrieval_day, post_retrieval]

## Preparing for retrieval day
[EDUCATION: retrieval_preparation]

Retrieval day can feel like a big deal — because it is. But it's also usually much simpler and quicker than people expect.

**The day before:**
- You'll typically need to fast from midnight (no food or water) because you'll be having sedation or light anaesthesia
- Take your trigger shot exactly when your clinic tells you — the timing is critical, down to the hour. Set multiple alarms
- Try to prepare things you'll want afterwards: comfortable clothes, a heat pack, your favourite comfort food, something gentle to watch
- Your feelings tonight might range from excited to terrified. Both make sense

**What to bring:**
- Your ID and any paperwork your clinic requires
- Comfortable, loose clothing (no tight waistbands)
- Warm socks (hospitals are cold)
- Your partner or support person
- Your phone and charger (there's often waiting time)

**What you don't need to bring:** Anxiety about being "strong." It's a medical procedure and you're allowed to feel however you feel about it.

## What happens during egg retrieval — step by step
[EDUCATION: retrieval_procedure]

The procedure itself usually takes only 15-30 minutes. Here's what happens:

1. **You arrive at the clinic** and change into a gown. A nurse will check your details and put in an IV line.

2. **Sedation:** Most clinics use conscious sedation (sometimes called "twilight") — you'll be drowsy and comfortable but not fully asleep. Some clinics use general anaesthesia. Either way, you won't feel pain during the procedure.

3. **The retrieval:** Your specialist uses an ultrasound-guided needle that passes through the vaginal wall to reach each follicle. They gently suction the fluid from each follicle — and hopefully, each one contains an egg. The embryologist checks the fluid immediately in the lab next door.

4. **You'll hear a count:** Often, the embryologist will call out each egg as they find it. Some people remember hearing this; others are too drowsy.

5. **Recovery:** You'll rest in the recovery area for 1-2 hours while the sedation wears off. You'll be offered tea, water, and something to eat.

6. **Going home:** You'll need someone to drive you home. Don't sign any important documents, make major decisions, or operate heavy machinery for 24 hours after sedation.

**A note about the number:** You'll probably know how many eggs were retrieved before you leave. Try not to panic if it's different from what you expected. Follicle count on ultrasound doesn't always equal egg count. Some follicles are empty, and sometimes there are pleasant surprises.

## Recovery after retrieval — what to expect
[EDUCATION: retrieval_recovery]

Most people feel okay within a day or two, but here's the honest timeline:

**Day 0 (retrieval day):**
- Cramping is normal — it can range from mild to moderate period-pain level
- Light spotting or bleeding is common
- You'll feel drowsy and a bit foggy from the sedation
- Rest, drink fluids, and be gentle with yourself
- A heat pack on your lower abdomen is your best friend

**Days 1-3:**
- Bloating may actually get worse before it gets better — your ovaries are still enlarged
- Mild cramping can continue
- Constipation is very common (blame the hormones and the procedure)
- You might feel emotionally fragile — this is the hormones plus the weight of what you're going through
- Most people take 1-2 days off work; listen to your body

**Days 3-7:**
- Things should gradually improve
- Bloating slowly reduces as your ovaries return to normal size
- You'll get your period in 1-2 weeks (unless you're doing a fresh transfer)

**What helps:** Water (lots of it), salty foods (they help with fluid balance), gentle walking, protein-rich meals, electrolyte drinks, and rest. This is not the time to push yourself.

**What to avoid:** Intense exercise, heavy lifting, baths or swimming (showers are fine), and sex — for at least a week, or as your clinic advises.

## OHSS — what to watch for
[EDUCATION: retrieval_ohss]

Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome (OHSS) happens when your ovaries over-respond to the stimulation medications. Mild OHSS is actually quite common (up to 33% of IVF cycles), but severe OHSS is rare (1-5%).

**Mild OHSS (common, usually manageable at home):**
- Moderate bloating and abdominal discomfort
- Mild nausea
- Slight weight gain

**Moderate to severe OHSS (call your clinic):**
- Severe bloating — your abdomen feels very tight and distended
- Significant pain
- Nausea or vomiting
- Reduced urination despite drinking normally
- Shortness of breath
- Rapid weight gain (more than 1kg per day)
- Dizziness or feeling faint

Your clinic monitors for OHSS risk throughout your cycle. If you had a high number of follicles or high oestradiol levels, they may have already adjusted your protocol to reduce the risk — for example, using a different trigger shot or recommending a "freeze all" (freezing all embryos instead of doing a fresh transfer).

If you're worried, call your clinic. They would much rather hear from you than have you sit at home anxious.
