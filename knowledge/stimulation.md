# Stimulation Phase
[STAGE: stimulation]

## What stimulation medications actually do in your body
[EDUCATION: stim_what_meds_do]

In a natural cycle, your body grows one dominant follicle and releases one egg. IVF stimulation medications override that system — they encourage your ovaries to grow multiple follicles at once, so your specialist can retrieve several eggs.

Here's what's happening inside:

**The injections (FSH/LH):** These are synthetic versions of hormones your body already makes. They're essentially turning up the volume on the signal that tells your follicles to grow. Instead of one follicle getting the "go ahead," several do.

**The blocker (antagonist or agonist):** At some point, you'll add a second injection that prevents your body from ovulating too early. Your body will naturally try to release an egg once the follicles reach a certain size — but we need them to stay put until retrieval day.

**What you might feel:** Bloating is very common — your ovaries are growing from walnut-sized to sometimes orange-sized. You might feel full, tired, emotional, or uncomfortable. Some people describe it as intense PMS. All of this is your body responding normally to the medications.

The whole stimulation phase typically lasts 8-14 days. It varies because everyone's ovaries respond differently — and that's okay.

## Managing injection anxiety — you're not alone in this
[EDUCATION: stim_injection_anxiety]

Let's be honest: giving yourself injections is not natural, and it's completely understandable to feel anxious, scared, or even a bit squeamish about it.

Here are some things that have helped other patients:

**Before you start:**
- Ask your clinic nurse to walk you through the first injection — most clinics will do this
- Watch one demonstration video, not ten. Too many can increase anxiety
- Prepare everything first: medication, alcohol swab, sharps container. Having it all ready makes it feel more manageable

**The injection itself:**
- Ice the area for 30 seconds beforehand — it numbs the skin and makes the needle less noticeable
- Pinch the skin firmly, insert quickly. Fast is better than slow
- Breathe out as you inject. Some people find counting to 3 helps
- It's okay to ask your partner to do it if that feels easier

**The emotional side:**
- Many people cry during their first injection. Some cry every time. Both are normal
- It often gets easier after the first few days — your body and brain adapt
- If the anxiety feels unmanageable, tell your clinic. They've seen it all and they can help

**A truth that helps:** The needles used for IVF injections are very small — much smaller than the ones used for blood tests. The anticipation is almost always worse than the actual injection.

## Side effects: what's normal vs when to call your clinic
[EDUCATION: stim_side_effects]

Your body is doing something extraordinary right now — growing multiple follicles instead of one. It's normal for it to protest a little.

**Normal side effects (common, expected):**
- Bloating and feeling "full" in your lower abdomen
- Mild cramping or tenderness near your ovaries
- Tiredness and low energy
- Mood swings and emotional sensitivity (the hormones are real)
- Mild headaches
- Feeling warmer than usual
- Slight weight gain from fluid retention
- Breast tenderness

**Talk to your clinic (not urgent, but worth mentioning):**
- Moderate discomfort that's hard to ignore
- Nausea that comes and goes
- Feeling more emotional than you can manage
- Constipation or changes in digestion
- Injection site that looks red or swollen

**Call your clinic urgently:**
- Severe abdominal pain or sudden sharp pain
- Vomiting that won't stop
- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
- Feeling dizzy or faint
- Very reduced urination despite drinking normally
- Rapid weight gain (more than 1kg per day)
- Swelling of your legs

These urgent symptoms could indicate OHSS (Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome) — a condition where your ovaries over-respond to the medication. It's treatable, but it needs medical attention. Don't wait and see — call your clinic.

## Why your monitoring scans matter
[EDUCATION: stim_monitoring]

During stimulation, you'll have regular ultrasound scans and blood tests — usually every 2-3 days, sometimes more often towards the end. Here's what your team is doing:

**The ultrasound** counts and measures your follicles. Each dark circle on the screen is a follicle, and inside each one is a potential egg. Your specialist is tracking how many are growing and how fast.

**The blood tests** check your oestradiol (estrogen) level, which rises as your follicles grow. This helps confirm that the follicles aren't just getting bigger — they're maturing properly.

**What they're looking for:** A "Goldilocks" response — not too few follicles (which might mean the medication dose needs increasing), not too many (which increases OHSS risk), and growth that's roughly even across the follicles.

**What if the numbers aren't what you expected?** Every cycle is different, and follicle counts on scans don't perfectly predict the number of eggs retrieved. Some follicles that look promising might be empty, and some smaller ones might surprise you. Try not to compare your numbers to anyone else's — your ovaries are unique.

Your specialist might adjust your medication dose based on these results. That's not a bad sign — it's precision medicine in action.
